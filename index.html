Military Dictatorships in SouthAmerica

How They Came to Power

Coups and who was behind them

In the 1960s and 1970s, many South American countries experienced military coups that removed their elected leaders. Starting with the 1964 coup in Brazil and followed by coups in both Chile and Uruguay in 1973, and then in Argentina in 1976, the Southern Cone in Latin America iincluding Bolivia, Ecuador, Paraguay, and Peru became a land of counterrevolutionary authoritarianism. The military leaders claimed they needed to fight against guerrilla groups and chaos to protect their countries and stop the spread of comunism.

But the leaders who were removed came from different political backgrounds. Some were left-wing socialists like Salvador Allende in Chile, while others were more central or right-wing like Juan MarÃ­a Bordaberry in Uruguay. On the other hand, guerrilla groups were active in many countries before the coups, such as the Montoneros in Argentina and Tupamaros in Uruguay, but in some cases, like Brazil and Peru, guerrilla movements grew stronger only after the military took power.

These coups were orchested not only by the military but also supported by powerful elites in each country. Many of the governments that were overthrown had promised to improve the lives of workers, women, and poor communities. For instance, in Brazil, GetÃºlio Vargas played a key role during the 1950s by building upon labor legislation and state-controlled unions,. In Argentina, Juan PerÃ³n emerged as one of the most charismatics populist leaders, serving from 1946 to 1955. Chile, however, had less experience with populist leadership. Instead, its traditional political parties broadened their appeal to include working-class voters. This political shift ultimately led to the election of Salvador Allende as president in 1970. For the elites, this was a threat to their wealth and control, which was built on systems of cheap labor and inequality.

Antidemocratic Practices

Repression and Human Rights Violations

Detainees during the military dictatorship in Argentina

During the 1960s to 1980s, several South American countries came under military dictatorships that ruled with extreme repression backed by the U.S.-influenced National Security Doctrine. These regimes positioned the armed forces as defenders of internal order and national security to suppress any form of political dissent. In Chile, Pinochet's regime relied on institutions like DINA (DirecciÃ³n de Inteligencia Nacional), the secret police, to carry out disappearances and torture. In Uruguay, the military and intelligence services worked together to arrest and persecute opponents. Across these countries, repression became state policy, with mass surveillance, censorship, and systematic use of violence to silence critics and eliminate supposed threats.

The military regimes carried out illegal arrests, kidnappings, and the forced disappearance of thousands of people. Victims were often held in secret detention centers, subjected to psychological and physical torture, and sometimes executed without trial. In Argentinaâ€™s "Dirty War" (1976â€“1983), around 30,000 people were disappeared. In Chile, more than 3,000 were killed or disappeared, and over 40,000 were tortured during Pinochetâ€™s rule. Brazil's dictatorship (1964â€“1985) left at least 434 dead or missing and thousands tortured, especially Indigenous people. In Uruguay, over 180 deaths were documented, and many more were detained and abused. These regimes created a climate of fear in which civil liberties were erased and even peaceful opposition was treated as criminal.

One of the most tragic aspects of these dictatorships was the systematic appropriation of children. In Argentina, babies born in captivity to disappeared political prisoners were taken from their mothers and illegally adopted, often by military families or regime supporters. Around 500 children grew up unaware of their true identity, and many cases remain unresolved today. Human rights organizations such as the Madres de Plaza de Mayo in Argentina have worked tirelessly to locate these children, and help them recover their identities.

Operation Condor

Transnational Repression and Cold War Influence

Operation Condor was a coordinated campaign of political repression carried out in the 1970s and 1980s by the military dictatorships of Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia, and Brazil. With support from the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency, this secret alliance allowed these regimes to share intelligence and execute surveillance, kidnappings, torture, forced disappearances, and assassinations across national borders. It targeted political opponents, including activists, leftist militants, students, union leaders, and exiles. One of the most infamous acts was the assassination of Chilean diplomat Orlando Letelier in Washington, D.C. in 1976. The scale of repression was massive: thousands of people were arrested or disappeared, often moved between countries through illegal transfers to avoid legal accountability. In total, Operation Condor resulted in an estimated 50,000 people killed, 30,000 disappeared, and 400,000 arrested.

To understand how such a transnational system of repression emerged, it is crucial to consider the broader geopolitical context. After World War II, Latin American countries faced a critical debate over the path to development. This debate intensified during the Cold War, as the United States sought to prevent the spread of communism and exerted pressure on Latin American governments to adopt free-market policies aligned with U.S. interests. Military coups across the region found strong support from Washington, which portrayed a wide range of ideologiesâ€”including populism, developmentalism, and nationalism, as forms of communism.

Consequently, the U.S. opposed figures across the political spectrum, from moderate developmentalists like RaÃºl Prebisch to populist nationalists like Juan PerÃ³n and GetÃºlio Vargas, as well as socialists like Salvador Allende. U.S. backing of military regimesâ€”through training, funding, and equipmentâ€”ultimately reinforced exclusionary social structures and fueled the violent repression of reformist movements, paving the way for operations like Condor.

In recent years, efforts have been made to uncover and document the full extent of Operation Condor's impact. One such initiative is the PlanCondor.org project, led by Francesca Lessa, a professor at the University of Oxford, in collaboration with organizations in Uruguay and Chile. This project has created a comprehensive online database featuring more than 800 victims. It reveals that nearly 40% of those targeted were political and social activistsâ€”countering the regimesâ€™ narrative that they were primarily armed guerrilla fighters. The platform offers detailed victim profiles, interactive maps tracing abductions and cross-border transfers, and a registry of judicial cases and verdicts, contributing to ongoing efforts for truth and justice.

Voices of Resistance

Memory and Justice Movements

In response to the repression and violence of the military dictatorships, powerful resistance and memory movements emerged across South America. In Argentina, the Mothers of the Plaza de Mayo began marching in 1977, demanding the return of their disappeared children. Their activism helped build a national movement that, over time, led to major trials and convictions of former military officials. Unlike other countries, Argentina has developed a strong state-supported process of memory, truth, and justice, especially over the past 20 years. This includes the reopening of trials, the establishment of memory sites, and educational programs to ensure that the history of the dictatorship remains present for future generations.

Other countries have also made significant efforts, though often with more obstacles. In Paraguay, Rogelio Goiburu, whose father was disappeared during Stroessnerâ€™s regime, has led investigations to recover remains and expose crimes through the country's Truth and Justice Commission. In Uruguay and Chile, justice efforts have advanced more slowly, often due to amnesty laws or political resistance, but human rights organizations have pushed for trials and worked to identify victims through forensic investigations.
